# Hypothesis Testing Framework for Current-Induced State Changes and Boundary Formation in TaSe$_2$ Nano-ARPES Data

## Introduction

The central scientific problem in the present dataset is not merely to identify where the sample appears metallic or insulating after a current pulse, but to determine which aspects of the local pre-pulse state control the subsequent evolution of the electronic structure. The observed patterns are spatially structured, history dependent, and entangled with non-electronic inhomogeneity arising from the morphology of the cleaved surface. A satisfactory analysis therefore cannot proceed by simple before-and-after inspection of intensity maps alone. It must instead be organized as a sequence of explicit hypotheses, each of which isolates one physical mechanism or one source of apparent contrast, and each of which can be tested quantitatively against the available data.

The purpose of this document is to lay out a rigorous program for testing such hypotheses. The emphasis is not only on what hypotheses are scientifically interesting, but on how to formulate them in a way that can actually be falsified with nano-ARPES maps, how to define the relevant observables, how to control for confounding effects such as local Brillouin-zone misregistration, and how to structure the full workflow so that conclusions about switching, erasing, and boundary formation are defensible.

The underlying difficulty is that several effects are superimposed in the same measurements. The electronic state itself changes under pulsing, as reflected in near-$E_F$ spectral weight, lower-Hubbard-band character, line shape, and dispersion. At the same time, the same sample contains multiple surface planes, local tilts, possible stacking variations, and internal domain boundaries that shift the effective momentum cut and alter the appearance of the spectra even in the absence of a real change in the local phase. In addition, spectra near real-space boundaries may represent mixtures of neighboring states within the spatial resolution of the beam. Any hypothesis-testing framework that does not explicitly address these three levels of variation—electronic, structural, and mixed-state—will tend to attribute too much significance to patterns that may be geometric in origin.

The scientific strategy must therefore be hierarchical. One first separates geometric and structural effects from genuine electronic variation as well as possible. One then identifies stable and reproducible electronic-state classes. Only after this step is it meaningful to ask what local quantities predict whether a region will switch into the metastable state, remain metallic, partially erase, or revert toward the ground state after an orthogonal pulse. The structure of the workflow should mirror that logic.

## General Philosophy of Hypothesis Testing in This Problem

A useful hypothesis in this context must satisfy several conditions simultaneously. It must refer to a physically meaningful quantity. It must make a prediction about an observable that can be extracted from the data. It must compete with at least one plausible alternative explanation. It must be testable across more than one pulse sequence or region of the sample. Finally, it must survive controls for local orientation, morphology, and beam-induced or contamination-induced artifacts.

This means that the analysis should not be organized around broad descriptive goals such as “find correlations” or “classify states” in the abstract. Rather, it should be organized around specific scientific claims of the form: the spatial probability that a pixel erases after an orthogonal pulse depends primarily on its distance to the metastable–CCDW boundary and not on its local spectrum alone; or, apparent pre-pulse spectral variability is dominated by momentum-cut misregistration and not by genuine differences in the electronic phase. Such claims can be denied by the data. That is precisely what makes them useful.

In practice, each hypothesis should be translated into a statistical comparison between two models. One model should represent the hypothesis under test. The other should represent a null explanation or a simpler alternative. The point is not to force the data into a prechosen conclusion, but to determine which class of explanation is genuinely required to account for the observed structure. In many cases the most important outcome may be that a commonly assumed explanation is insufficient.

The entire program also requires careful separation of exploratory and confirmatory analysis. Exploratory analysis is necessary in the early stages because the relevant descriptors of the local spectra may not be known in advance. However, once a promising feature or predictive relation has been identified, it should be carried forward into a confirmatory test on different pulse cycles, different spatial windows, or different samples. Otherwise, it is too easy to mistake overfitted pattern recognition for real mechanism.

## Precondition for All Downstream Hypotheses: Structural Registration and Electronic Normalization

Before any physical hypothesis about state switching or boundary behavior can be tested, the dataset must be brought into a form in which different pixels can be compared meaningfully. The reason is straightforward. Two spectra can differ because the material is electronically different at those two points, but they can also differ because the local surface is tilted and the ARPES cut is shifted in momentum space. In the latter case, a comparison of gap size, band position, or near-$E_F$ intensity taken at nominally identical momentum coordinates is misleading because the spectra are not actually taken along the same path through the Brillouin zone.

The first stage of the full approach must therefore produce a local registration map. At each spatial coordinate, one should estimate the effective momentum offset, local cut orientation, and, if possible, whether the local cut is better described as one chiral or mirrored variant of the same reconstructed domain. The precise method can vary, but the result should be a transformation that allows each spectrum to be represented in a common momentum reference frame. This does not eliminate all structural effects, since matrix-element variations and resolution changes may remain, but it removes the largest source of artificial spectral diversity.

Only once this registration is complete should the core electronic descriptors be computed. These descriptors should include quantities closely related to the known physics of the problem, such as near-$E_F$ spectral weight, leading-edge position, lower-Hubbard-band position and width, the presence or absence of shoulders indicative of intermediate states, and measures of spectral curvature or dispersion near the expected gap region. The purpose of registration is not aesthetic. It is the condition required for any subsequent test of whether the local electronic structure predicts future switching behavior.

For this reason, the first hypothesis to be tested is not about switching at all. It concerns the origin of spectral variability in the as-measured data.

## Hypothesis 1: Most Apparent Pre-Pulse Spectral Variability Is Geometric Rather Than Electronic

The first hypothesis to test is that most of the spatial variation seen before pulsing, or in apparently similar regions after pulsing, is caused by local differences in surface orientation, momentum-cut placement, or other structural registration effects, rather than by genuine differences in the local electronic state. This is foundational because, if true, it means that a substantial fraction of apparent state diversity is not physically relevant to switching. If false, then one must take seriously the possibility that multiple distinct electronic configurations coexist on the sample even before pulsing.

The logic of the test is as follows. One first quantifies spectral variability in the raw data, for example by computing the variance of normalized spectra across spatial coordinates, or by measuring the spread of physically interpretable features such as band positions and apparent gap values. One then performs the best possible local momentum registration and recomputes the same variability measures in the registered frame. If the dominant source of variability is geometric, then a substantial fraction of the variance should collapse after registration. Spectra that previously appeared different should align more closely, especially in features expected to be sensitive to cut position but not to intrinsic phase.

This test can be formulated more rigorously by fitting two competing models to the dataset. In one model, each local spectrum is described as a transformed version of a small number of underlying electronic templates, where the transformations account for momentum shift, local rotation, and possibly smooth intensity remapping. In the second model, the same data are fit using multiple unconstrained electronic templates without any explicit geometric transformation. If the first model explains the data nearly as well with fewer truly distinct electronic endmembers, then geometric variation is dominant. If the second model is substantially better even after registration, then genuine electronic diversity is present.

The practical structure of the test should include a training stage in which the registration procedure is tuned and a held-out stage in which the reduction in spectral variance is evaluated on regions or pulse scans not used in tuning. This prevents the registration from simply memorizing noise patterns. A useful secondary check is to inspect whether features known to be orientation-sensitive, such as band positions in regions of steep dispersion, show a stronger variance collapse than features tied directly to the local electronic phase, such as integrated near-$E_F$ intensity.

If the hypothesis is supported, then all downstream analyses should be performed in the registered coordinate system. If it is not supported, then one must instead explicitly model multiple coexisting electronic states already at the earliest stage.

## Hypothesis 2: After Structural Registration, the Sample Contains a Finite Number of Reproducible Electronic-State Classes

Once structural variability has been reduced as much as possible, the next question is whether the remaining spectra fall into a finite set of reproducible electronic-state classes. This hypothesis is motivated by the repeated appearance of spectra that are intuitively described as CCDW-like, metastable metallic, partially erased, or anomalous metallic patch states. However, intuitive labels are not enough. One must determine whether the data genuinely support stable classes or instead exhibit a continuous manifold of spectral evolution.

The relevant test begins by embedding the registered spectra in a lower-dimensional representation while preserving the physically meaningful line-shape differences. One may use principal components, autoencoder latent coordinates, or another embedding method, but the important point is that the representation should be smooth enough to reveal structure and interpretable enough to reconstruct mean spectra. Clustering should then be performed in this reduced space, and the stability of the resulting clusters must be evaluated across repeated runs, different subsets of the data, and different pulse scans.

The key issue is not whether any clustering algorithm can partition the data, since one can always force a partition. The issue is whether there exist spectral classes whose mean spectra are physically distinct, whose spatial distributions are coherent, and whose membership is reproducible under small perturbations of the analysis. A class should not be accepted as meaningful unless it satisfies these requirements. If the number and character of the clusters change wildly under modest changes in preprocessing or subsampling, the data are better interpreted as continuous variation.

This hypothesis can be tested against an alternative description in which local spectra evolve continuously along one or two principal axes, for example from more insulating to more metallic, without discrete phase classes. One way to compare these pictures is to ask whether classification into a small number of states predicts future switching behavior better than continuous descriptors alone. If discrete labels have substantially stronger predictive value and remain stable across pulse sequences, then they capture meaningful physics. If continuous descriptors suffice, then a class-based description may be an unnecessary simplification.

A particularly important case concerns the pre-existing metallic patch. If this patch is simply a partially mixed version of the same metastable metallic state written by current pulses, then its spectra should lie on a path connecting the CCDW and written-metastable classes. If instead its dispersion character remains distinct even after structural registration, then it must be treated as a separate electronic configuration, possibly related to stacking or local structural reconstruction.

The outcome of this stage should be a set of well-defined spectral descriptors or class labels that can be carried into predictive tests. One should not proceed to “what causes erasing” before establishing what the states actually are.

## Hypothesis 3: Switching and Erasing Depend on the Local Pre-Pulse Electronic State

After the pre-pulse spectra have been registered and represented in a physically meaningful way, one can test whether the fate of a pixel under a given pulse is controlled by its local pre-pulse electronic structure. This is one of the most natural hypotheses in the problem. If certain regions are more likely to switch to the metastable state, or more likely to erase under an orthogonal pulse, because they begin in a particular spectral condition, then the local spectrum contains predictive information about the transition pathway.

The relevant observable is not the spectral class itself but the state change induced by a pulse. One must therefore define outcome variables carefully. Depending on the scientific focus, the outcome may be binary, such as whether a pixel becomes metallic after a pulse, or multinomial, such as whether it remains CCDW-like, becomes metastable metallic, becomes an intermediate state, or partially erases. Continuous outcome measures may also be useful, for example the change in $I_{\mathrm{rat}}$, the change in leading-edge position, or the change in membership probability for a metastable-state cluster.

To test the hypothesis, predictive models should be built using only pre-pulse spectral descriptors as inputs. These models must be evaluated on held-out pulse transitions. If local pre-pulse electronic structure is genuinely informative, prediction accuracy should exceed what can be achieved using only global quantities or naive spatial priors. Importantly, the models should be compared against alternatives that use structural information only. This is because local spectral differences can still encode local orientation or morphology indirectly, even after registration. A convincing result requires showing that electronic descriptors retain predictive power after controlling for these variables.

The strongest form of the hypothesis would be that specific spectral signatures precede switching in a physically interpretable way. For example, a region with a slightly filled gap or a residual shoulder near the lower-Hubbard-band energy might act as a precursor configuration that more easily converts into the metastable state upon the next pulse. An analogous argument applies to erasing. If partially erased regions never return fully to the original CCDW spectrum and instead retain weak metallic spectral weight, such regions may serve as memory-bearing precursors for re-switching.

The test should therefore not stop at prediction accuracy. It should also include interpretation of which spectral features are most predictive and whether those features match physically plausible notions of partial phase conversion, residual discommensurations, or incipient metallicity. If the predictive power of the local spectrum is weak after proper controls, then one must conclude that the fate of a region is governed primarily by neighborhood or structural factors rather than by the local spectrum alone.

## Hypothesis 4: Switching and Erasing Depend More Strongly on the Local Neighborhood Than on the Pixel Alone

The data suggest that current-induced state changes are not purely local. Metallic regions are spatially extended, erasing appears strongest near interfaces, and the persistence of memory implies that the surrounding environment matters. This motivates a hypothesis stronger than the previous one: the probability that a region switches or erases is controlled not only by its own local spectrum but by the state of the surrounding neighborhood.

This hypothesis should be tested by constructing descriptors that summarize the local spatial environment before the pulse. These descriptors may include the fraction of nearby pixels belonging to each electronic class, the local gradient of metallicity, the distance to the nearest metastable–CCDW interface, the local curvature of that interface, and neighborhood averages of structural-orientation descriptors. These quantities should be defined over several length scales to determine whether the relevant influence is nearest-neighbor-like or extends over tens of microns.

The cleanest test compares two predictive models. In the first, only the local pixel’s own descriptors are used. In the second, neighborhood descriptors are added. If the second model yields a robust and statistically significant improvement on held-out data, then the dynamics are genuinely nonlocal at the scale of the chosen descriptors. The physical interpretation would be that the transition probability is controlled by the local environment, perhaps through current redistribution, heat flow, or interfacial nucleation processes.

There is an additional conceptual benefit to this hypothesis. It provides a way to distinguish phase-boundary-mediated dynamics from purely local threshold behavior. If switching or erasing were determined by a simple local threshold in a hidden scalar variable, then neighborhood descriptors would not add much once the local spectrum and structure were known. If, on the other hand, interfaces are active regions where the state can retreat or regrow, then distances to interfaces and measures of neighborhood composition should become dominant predictors.

This test should be carried out separately for writing and erasing transitions. It is entirely possible that the creation of a metastable region is mostly determined by the local current path and threshold crossing, whereas the erasing of an existing region is controlled far more strongly by its interface geometry and surrounding thermal environment. Treating the two transitions as the same process would obscure this possibility.

## Hypothesis 5: Erasing Is Strongest at the Metastable–CCDW Interface Rather Than in the Interior of Metallic Regions

A particularly important hypothesis, and one already strongly suggested by qualitative inspection, is that erasing under an orthogonal pulse is concentrated near the interface between the written metastable region and the surrounding CCDW-like state. This hypothesis matters because it directly constrains the mechanism of erasing. If erasing occurs uniformly throughout the metallic interior, then the pulse may be acting as a spatially broad destabilizing field. If erasing occurs primarily at the interface, then interface-mediated retreat, current crowding, or thermal bottleneck effects become far more plausible.

To test this properly, one must first segment the pre-erase state into metastable and non-metastable regions using either a thresholded continuous descriptor or a probabilistic state classifier derived from the earlier analysis. From this segmentation one computes the signed distance of every pixel to the nearest phase boundary, with one sign for pixels inside the metastable region and the opposite sign for pixels outside. One then measures the average change induced by the erasing pulse as a function of this distance.

If the hypothesis is correct, the erasing signal should peak at or very near zero signed distance, meaning at the interface itself, and decay as one moves into the metallic interior. The scale of that decay is physically meaningful, since it provides an estimate of how far the destabilizing effect penetrates into the metastable region at the spatial resolution of the experiment. This should be tested across multiple pulse cycles and compared against a null model in which the same total amount of erasing is distributed randomly or uniformly across the previously metallic area.

A stronger test includes control for the shape of the interface. If erasing is purely tied to distance from the interface, then interface curvature should not matter much. If highly curved or geometrically constricted regions erase more strongly, then this suggests a role for current concentration, thermal accumulation, or interfacial line tension. Such distinctions are important, because several mechanisms can produce “more erasing at boundaries” in a qualitative sense while differing in how geometry enters quantitatively.

This hypothesis also provides one of the clearest bridges between data analysis and later simulation. A measured erasing profile in signed-distance coordinates gives a concrete target for thermal or interface-kinetics models to reproduce.

## Hypothesis 6: Structural Boundaries Modulate Erasing Independently of Electronic Phase Boundaries

The observed erasing pattern appears grid-like and often aligns with visible or inferred real-space boundaries. The crucial question is whether these structural boundaries matter only because they frequently coincide with electronic phase boundaries, or whether they independently modulate the erasing process. Distinguishing these possibilities is essential. If structural boundaries have an independent effect, then morphology is not merely a nuisance variable but part of the mechanism.

The test requires two separate maps of boundaries. The first is the electronic phase-boundary map derived from the segmentation of metastable versus CCDW-like regions. The second is a structural-boundary map derived from optical microscopy, local orientation changes inferred from the ARPES registration, or both. Once these maps are available, one can define for each pixel its distance to the nearest structural boundary and its distance to the nearest electronic phase boundary. These distances can then be used together in a joint statistical model of erasing magnitude or erase probability.

If structural boundaries have no independent role, then once distance to the electronic boundary is included, distance to the structural boundary should add little explanatory power. If, however, the structural-boundary variable remains significant, then erasing is being modulated by morphology in a way that cannot be explained solely by the location of the phase interface. In physical terms, this would suggest that terraces, tilts, layer steps, flake boundaries, or other real-space inhomogeneities alter current flow, heat dissipation, or local stability.

This test is especially important because simple visual overlap is not enough. In these datasets, many structural and electronic boundaries partially coincide. Only a joint analysis can separate their contributions. One should therefore avoid qualitative statements of the form “the erasing lines seem to follow boundaries” unless the independent contribution of structural boundaries has been quantified.

## Hypothesis 7: The Pre-Existing Metallic Patch Is a Distinct Metastable Configuration Rather Than a Partial Mixture of Ground and Written States

The anomalous metallic patch observed prior to pulsing is scientifically important because it may represent a distinct local configuration of the material rather than a trivial admixture of the two more familiar states. If this is true, then the local energy landscape contains more than one relevant metastable basin, and the pulse-driven dynamics may connect these basins in different ways depending on location and morphology.

The test should proceed by comparing the spectra from the patch to spectra from well-defined CCDW and written-metastable regions after all reasonable structural registration has been performed. The key quantities are not only integrated metallicity measures such as near-$E_F$ weight but the full line shape and dispersion character. If the patch can be reconstructed accurately as a convex combination of CCDW-like and written-metastable spectra, then it need not be treated as an independent state. If, however, reconstruction errors remain systematic and physically coherent—for example through a qualitatively different near-$E_F$ dispersion sign or the persistence of a feature absent in both endpoint states—then the patch constitutes a separate class.

This hypothesis can also be tested dynamically. If the patch were only a partial mixture of the endpoint states, then under pulsing it should evolve along the same path as ordinary pixels with similar metallicity. If instead it responds differently, such as converting preferentially into one type of intermediate state or erasing in an anomalous fashion, then it must be treated as a distinct precursor configuration. This dynamical criterion is powerful because it uses the pulse sequence itself to probe whether the patch occupies a separate branch of the phase landscape.

A positive result here would justify a substantial shift in the conceptual framing of the project. Instead of a single written state and a single erased state, the correct picture would involve multiple metastable electronic configurations whose accessibility depends on local structure.

## Hypothesis 8: Writing and Erasing Are Governed by Different Physical Descriptors

It is tempting to think of writing and erasing as inverse processes, but the data do not require this. The written metallic bar is comparatively homogeneous and sharply bounded, whereas erasing under an orthogonal pulse appears spatially selective and strongly boundary-modulated. This suggests that the mechanisms governing writing and erasing may be different even if both are ultimately caused by current pulses.

The appropriate hypothesis is that the descriptors that best predict writing differ from those that best predict erasing. Writing may depend most strongly on descriptors related to current-path geometry, initial resistance, and threshold crossing. Erasing may depend more strongly on phase-boundary distance, local neighborhood composition, or thermal bottlenecks created by surrounding insulating regions. To test this, one should build separate predictive models for writing transitions and erasing transitions using the same candidate feature set, and then compare the learned feature importance, generalization performance, and optimal model structure.

A strong outcome would be that the best writing model relies primarily on pre-pulse local or structural descriptors, while the best erasing model relies primarily on interface and neighborhood descriptors. Such a result would substantially narrow the plausible mechanism space. It would argue against oversimplified single-parameter descriptions in which both processes are controlled by the same local hidden variable.

This hypothesis also has organizational consequences. It means that the analysis pipeline should treat writing and erasing as separate tasks from the outset rather than combining them into one generic “state change” model.

## Structuring the Full Approach

The overall approach should be organized as a staged inference pipeline in which each stage produces a well-defined output consumed by the next. The first stage ingests the raw spatially resolved ARPES cubes and associated microscope or metadata information and performs calibration, normalization, and local structural registration. The output of this stage is a spatial map of spectra represented in a common momentum frame together with orientation descriptors and quality-control masks.

The second stage extracts electronic descriptors and constructs a representation of local electronic state. This stage may produce both continuous features and discrete probabilistic state labels. The essential requirement is that the representation remain tied to physically interpretable spectral content.

The third stage identifies phase boundaries and structural boundaries in a unified spatial coordinate system. From these it derives geometric descriptors such as interface distance, structural-boundary distance, local curvature, neighborhood composition, and morphological context. These descriptors are crucial for testing the nonlocal and boundary-mediated hypotheses.

The fourth stage carries out predictive and comparative hypothesis tests for individual pulse transitions. Each transition should be represented as a mapping from a pre-pulse state map to a post-pulse outcome map. Models should then be trained and evaluated using only the descriptors available before the pulse. In this way one avoids leakage of post-transition information into the prediction task.

The fifth stage synthesizes results across pulse sequences and samples. A hypothesis should not be regarded as supported unless the corresponding effect is stable across more than one transition or sample geometry. This is particularly important because morphology can vary strongly from sample to sample and because a model can appear persuasive on a single pulse pair while failing completely on the next one.

The entire workflow should maintain a strict record of preprocessing choices, registration parameters, feature definitions, model versions, train-test splits, and evaluation metrics. In a project of this kind, reproducibility is not a bureaucratic detail. It is necessary because the conclusions will inevitably depend on nuanced distinctions between structural correction, state representation, and spatial modeling.

## How to Evaluate the Tests

Each hypothesis should have an evaluation criterion matched to its scientific content. For registration hypotheses, the appropriate measures are variance reduction, alignment error, and cross-validated reconstruction quality. For state-class hypotheses, the relevant metrics include cluster stability, spatial coherence, reconstruction residuals, and predictive usefulness for later transitions. For predictive hypotheses about writing or erasing, the key metrics are held-out classification or regression performance, calibration of probabilities, and the incremental value of added descriptor families such as neighborhood or boundary terms.

However, numerical performance alone is not enough. Every supported hypothesis should also yield a physically interpretable result. If a model predicts erasing accurately but only through opaque latent variables that cannot be mapped back to spectral or spatial structure, its scientific value is limited. This does not mean complex models should be avoided, but it does mean that they should be accompanied by post hoc analysis showing what physical information they are using.

Evaluation should also include negative controls. For example, one can test whether the same predictors appear significant when the spatial arrangement of the outcome map is randomly permuted or when a pulse transition is paired with the wrong preceding state map. A hypothesis that remains “supported” under such controls is likely responding to trivial dataset biases rather than causal structure.

## How the Hypotheses Fit Together

These hypotheses are not independent. They form a chain of scientific refinement. The first question is whether the raw spectral variation is dominated by geometry. The second is whether, once corrected, the sample contains reproducible electronic-state classes. The third is whether the local pre-pulse state predicts future evolution. The fourth and fifth ask whether state evolution is fundamentally boundary- and neighborhood-mediated rather than purely local. The sixth determines whether morphology has an independent role. The seventh asks whether the system contains more than one metastable configuration. The eighth determines whether writing and erasing belong to the same or different dynamical families.

The value of structuring the project in this way is that failure at one stage is informative rather than catastrophic. If local electronic state does not predict switching, that does not invalidate the project. It redirects attention toward nonlocal transport or morphology. If structural registration removes little of the apparent variability, that itself is a major physical result, because it means the material hosts more genuine electronic diversity than initially assumed. A careful hypothesis-testing framework turns uncertainty into a productive guide rather than a weakness.

## Conclusion

The correct way to analyze these nano-ARPES pulse-sequence data is not to begin with a machine-learning method and ask what it finds, but to begin with a set of explicit physical hypotheses and then design the analysis so that each hypothesis can succeed or fail on the basis of observable evidence. The dataset is rich enough to support this approach, but only if the workflow is disciplined. Structural registration, electronic-state representation, boundary extraction, and predictive modeling must be treated as sequential inferential tasks rather than as loosely connected visual analyses.

The most important scientific question is what local or nonlocal variables determine whether a region writes, persists, erases, or becomes intermediate under successive current pulses. Answering that question requires separating electronic effects from structural artifacts, distinguishing distinct electronic configurations from mixtures, and testing whether the decisive predictors lie in the local spectrum, the local neighborhood, the phase boundary geometry, or the morphology of the sample. A rigorous hypothesis-driven framework is the clearest route toward that answer.
