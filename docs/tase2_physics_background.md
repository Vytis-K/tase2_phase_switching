# Physics Background for Interpreting Current-Pulse-Induced State Changes and Boundary Formation in TaSe$_2$ ARPES Data

## Introduction

The purpose of this document is to provide a physics framework for interpreting spatially resolved ARPES data acquired on a pulsed TaSe$_2$ device in which current pulses write, erase, and reshape long-lived electronic states. The emphasis is on the physical concepts that are most important for reading the data correctly before attempting modeling or simulation. In practice, the central challenge is that several distinct effects can produce superficially similar changes in the measured spectra. A local spectrum may change because the underlying electronic state has changed, because the local crystal orientation changes the momentum cut that the analyzer samples, or because the measurement averages over a spatial boundary between two neighboring phases or structural regions. The interpretation of the data therefore requires a careful separation of electronic, structural, and geometric effects.

This separation is especially important in layered charge-density-wave materials such as TaSe$_2$, where the electronic structure is strongly coupled to lattice reconstruction, local stacking, surface morphology, and thermal history. The current-pulse experiment is not simply probing a static equilibrium band structure. It is probing a non-equilibrium system that can be driven into long-lived metastable configurations and then partially returned toward a more insulating state by later pulses along another direction. The relevant physics therefore includes charge-density-wave reconstruction, gap formation, spectral-weight transfer, metastability, boundary motion, current and heat flow in an inhomogeneous medium, and the effect of local crystallographic orientation on the measured Brillouin-zone cut.

The discussion below is organized to build the conceptual foundation needed to look at the data and recognize which signatures are likely to reflect real electronic changes and which are more likely to arise from structural or geometric effects.

## Charge-density waves and why they matter in ARPES

A charge-density wave is a state in which the electronic density and the lattice develop a periodic modulation with a wavevector that differs from the original lattice periodicity. In a real material this should not be thought of as purely a modulation of charge with the ions acting as passive spectators. The electronic instability and the lattice distortion are coupled, so the charge-density wave corresponds to a new broken-symmetry state of the crystal in which the translational symmetry is reduced relative to the high-symmetry parent structure.

When the translational symmetry changes, the size and shape of the effective unit cell also change. In reciprocal space this means that the Brillouin zone of the reconstructed state is smaller than that of the unreconstructed lattice. Bands that in the original description were distinct can then be folded back into the reduced zone. This immediately has observable consequences in ARPES. One expects the appearance of folded dispersions, replica or shadow features, changes in spectral weight at symmetry-related momenta, and altered band crossings or avoided crossings where the new periodic potential mixes states that were previously unrelated.

In a strongly reconstructed material the change is not only geometric in reciprocal space. The reconstructed potential can strongly reorganize the low-energy electronic structure. Bands can split, flatten, or hybridize, and spectral weight can be shifted away from the Fermi level. If electronic correlations are strong, the reconstructed state may also acquire a pronounced gap or a lower-energy incoherent feature resembling a lower Hubbard band. For this reason one must avoid the simplistic idea that the charge-density wave is just an extra weak superlattice modulation that produces small copies of the main bands. In materials of the TaS$_2$/TaSe$_2$ family, the charge-density wave can qualitatively reorganize the low-energy spectrum.

This point is central for the present experiment. A current pulse is believed to transform parts of the sample from a more insulating charge-density-wave-dominated state into a metastable metallic state. In ARPES this will not merely appear as a rigid movement of all bands. The physically relevant signatures are redistribution of spectral weight near the Fermi level, changes in gap size or gap filling, movement or reshaping of lower-energy features, and possibly changes in the visibility of reconstructed or folded bands. Interpreting the data therefore requires familiarity with how a charge-density-wave state manifests itself both through its reconstructed Brillouin zone and through the energy-resolved distribution of spectral weight.

## What it means for a region to be metallic or insulating in the present data

In a simple metal, ARPES intensity reaches the Fermi level in a way consistent with occupied states crossing into the chemical potential. In a simple band insulator, by contrast, one expects no spectral weight at the Fermi level because the valence band maximum lies below it and the conduction band minimum lies above it. In a correlated charge-density-wave material, however, the distinction between metallic and insulating is often more subtle. The low-energy spectral function can show partial suppression of spectral weight, a pseudogap, a strongly broadened leading edge, or coexistence of coherent and incoherent features. The question is not simply whether the measured spectrum has any counts at the Fermi level, but how the spectral weight is distributed in a narrow energy window near the Fermi level relative to characteristic lower-energy peaks.

For this reason near-Fermi-level weight is often converted into a robust contrast metric such as an intensity ratio between spectral weight close to the Fermi level and spectral weight at a lower-energy reference peak. Such a quantity is useful because it is less sensitive than full peak fitting to local broadening, weak contamination, or imperfect modeling of the line shape. However, one must understand precisely what such a metric is diagnosing. It is not a fundamental order parameter. It is a proxy for the extent to which the low-energy spectrum looks gap-filled or metallic. Two regions can have similar intensity ratios while still differing in the detailed shape of the low-energy band, in the momentum dependence of the gap, or in the sign of the near-Fermi-level dispersion. Conversely, two regions can have different ratios partly because a structural effect has moved the measured cut through momentum space, causing a band edge to appear closer to or farther from the Fermi level even if the intrinsic local state is the same.

A careful interpretation of metallicity in the present data should therefore combine at least three considerations. The first is the integrated spectral weight near the Fermi level. The second is the energy position and shape of characteristic lower-energy features such as the lower Hubbard band or related reconstructed valence features. The third is the momentum dependence of the near-Fermi-level signal. A truly metallic metastable state is not only expected to show increased near-Fermi-level intensity, but also a low-energy structure whose dispersion and line shape are characteristic of that state. A region that only appears more metallic because the local surface orientation has shifted the momentum cut should not be assumed to represent a distinct electronic phase.

## How a charge-density-wave state appears in ARPES

The most direct electronic signature of a charge-density-wave state is suppression or removal of spectral weight at the Fermi level over the parts of momentum space that are affected by the reconstruction. In practice this can appear as an energy gap, a partial gap, a leading-edge shift away from the Fermi level, or strong loss of coherent spectral weight in a narrow energy window near the chemical potential. The details depend on the material and on whether the ordered state is weakly coupled, strongly correlated, fully commensurate, or only partially ordered.

A second signature is the presence of reconstructed dispersions. Because the charge-density wave introduces a new periodicity, states separated by the ordering wavevector can hybridize. One therefore expects spectral features to appear at momenta where they would not be present in the unreconstructed lattice description. These features are often weaker than the main bands and may only be visible after averaging, denoising, or careful background subtraction. In a spatially resolved experiment they may be especially difficult to detect because neighboring points on the sample can have slightly different orientations, causing the same reconstructed feature to move in and out of the measured cut.

A third signature is redistribution of spectral weight among low-energy bands. Even when the gross dispersion remains recognizable, the relative intensity of peaks can change substantially across a charge-density-wave transition or across a transition from the ground state to a metastable state. One common mistake is to judge the state of a region only from the location of one peak. In a reconstructed correlated material the more informative quantity is often how the entire low-energy line shape changes. The relevant questions include whether weight has moved toward the Fermi level, whether a shoulder has appeared on a pre-existing peak, whether a broad incoherent tail has developed, and whether the spectrum resembles a mixture of two neighboring phases.

If the current-written state is indeed a distinct metastable metallic phase, one should expect the measured spectra to differ from the initial charge-density-wave state in all three senses just described. There should be more low-energy weight, changes in the detailed lineshape of the low-energy peaks, and possibly altered visibility of reconstructed states or backfolded features. If only the apparent momentum location of a band changes while the low-energy spectral-weight redistribution does not, then the observation is more likely to be structural or geometric rather than a genuine electronic transformation.

## The Brillouin zone, local orientation, and why geometry can imitate physics

Spatially resolved ARPES on a rough or multi-domain cleaved surface is not equivalent to collecting the same momentum cut at every spatial pixel. Each point on the sample can have a slightly different local orientation relative to the analyzer and incoming beam. Even small tilts can shift which slice through the local Brillouin zone is being measured. As a result, the same underlying electronic structure can generate noticeably different ARPES images from different points on the sample.

This problem is especially acute when the quantity of interest is determined from a narrow momentum window or when the low-energy features are strongly momentum dependent. A local tilt can shift an occupied band closer to the nominal momentum point at which the analysis is performed, making the spectrum appear more metallic or less gapped than it intrinsically is. It can also move a band maximum or minimum in momentum space, broaden the apparent feature after integration, and change matrix-element weights. None of these effects necessarily implies that the local electronic state is different.

It is important to distinguish this local-orientation effect from a true change in the Brillouin-zone reconstruction associated with a change of phase. A local tilt or rotation generally produces a geometric remapping of the same bands. The principal effect is that the measured cut moves in momentum space. A true change in the electronic state, by contrast, changes the content of the spectral function itself. New folded features may appear, existing features may disappear or hybridize, the gap may open or fill, and the relative spectral weights may change in a way that cannot be explained by a rigid shift of the cut.

The practical consequence is that one of the most important conceptual tasks in the analysis is to infer, for each spatial point, where the measured spectrum lies within the local Brillouin zone. If this local registration can be estimated, the spectra can be transformed into a common momentum-space frame. Only then can one ask in a controlled way whether there are residual spectral differences that correspond to real electronic distinctions. Without such a correction, structural domain boundaries and changes in local flake tilt can easily masquerade as phase boundaries.

## Structural domains, terraces, flakes, and internal orientation boundaries

A cleaved layered crystal rarely presents a single, atomically flat, uniform surface over the full field of view of a nano-ARPES scan. More commonly the surface contains terraces, steps between layers of different height, flakes with slightly different tilt or azimuthal orientation, and internal kinks within nominally single surface planes. All of these features can imprint themselves on the ARPES data.

Some structural boundaries are obvious in an optical or electron microscope image because they coincide with visible steps or edges. Others are harder to identify directly in real space but can still be inferred from ARPES because they produce systematic momentum shifts across the boundary. For example, if one peak or band position is particularly sensitive to local orientation, a spatial map of that quantity can reveal internal domain boundaries within a surface plane that are not evident in the microscope image. Such boundaries are structurally significant even if they do not themselves correspond to a change in electronic phase.

This distinction matters because the pulse-induced patterns may interact with these structural features in two different ways. First, the current and heat flow can be redistributed by the morphology of the sample. A terrace edge or flake boundary may change local conductivity or thermal dissipation and thereby affect where a written or erased region forms. Second, the ARPES signatures near such a boundary can become harder to interpret because geometric changes across the boundary can alter the observed spectra even in the absence of a true phase change. The same line in a spatial map can therefore have both a physical role in shaping the current-pulse response and a measurement role in modifying how the local electronic structure is projected into the data.

For this reason the words boundary and domain should always be used carefully. In the present context, a boundary can mean a boundary between two structural surface regions, a boundary between two electronic phases, or a line where both coincide. A domain can mean an area with approximately uniform crystallographic orientation, an area with approximately uniform electronic structure, or a region that is homogeneous in both senses. A major part of the physics interpretation consists in determining which sense is relevant for each observed feature.

## Metastability and why the pulse-written state is not just a heated equilibrium phase

The pulse-written state is described as metastable rather than simply metallic because it persists after the pulse ends, often for a long time compared with microscopic relaxation times. This indicates that the system is not merely following the equilibrium phase diagram as a function of instantaneous temperature. Instead, the current pulse drives the material across an energy barrier into a different local minimum of its free-energy landscape. Once there, the system can remain trapped even after the external drive has been removed.

This language of metastability is important because it changes how one interprets both writing and erasing. If the experiment were only heating and cooling the sample through an equilibrium transition, then after the pulse the state would simply be determined by the final local temperature. The persistence of a long-lived non-equilibrium state implies that history matters. The state at a given point depends not only on the local conditions during the latest pulse but also on the prior pulse sequence, the pre-existing domain structure, and the accessibility of transition pathways.

In such a system, a later pulse along an orthogonal direction need not return a region fully to the original ground state. It may instead produce an intermediate configuration that is electronically closer to the insulating state but still retains memory of the previously written phase. In ARPES this kind of incomplete erasure can appear as residual near-Fermi-level weight, a shifted or broadened lower-energy peak, or a shoulder that persists after the nominally erasing pulse. This observation is physically significant. It suggests that the erase process is not simply the reverse of the write process and that the path through configuration space matters.

The concept of metastability also clarifies why one should not assume that every metallic-looking region represents the same state. Distinct metastable configurations can exist with similar integrated low-energy weight but different detailed dispersions, different local stacking arrangements, or different coupling to the underlying lattice. If the data contain multiple kinds of metallic or partially metallic spectra, the interpretation should not begin from the assumption that all of them are merely varying fractions of one universal metallic phase.

## Nucleation, growth, and boundary motion

A first-order or strongly hysteretic transition in a spatially extended system does not usually proceed by every point changing state simultaneously. Instead, one phase nucleates in favorable regions and then grows. Once two phases coexist in neighboring regions, an interface forms between them. The motion of this interface depends on the local energetic driving force, the barrier for conversion, and the ease with which heat, current, strain, or other conserved quantities can move through the surrounding medium.

This framework is directly relevant to interpreting written and erased regions in the spatial maps. A written metallic bar should not be thought of only as a collection of isolated pixels with enhanced near-Fermi-level weight. It is a finite region of one phase embedded in another, with an interface that may be physically important. The same is true during erasure. If erasure appears strongest at the outer boundary of a metallic region rather than in its center, that suggests that the interface is a privileged location for phase conversion. There are several physically plausible reasons for this. The interface may be where the alternative phase can most easily grow. The current density or heat dissipation may be enhanced there. The local free-energy balance may differ because neighboring insulating and metallic regions conduct heat and charge differently.

The key point is that the interface is not merely a geometric afterthought. It can actively control the kinetics of both writing and erasing. Once this is appreciated, a variety of observations become easier to interpret. A sharp boundary between a metallic and insulating region is evidence that the conversion threshold is relatively well defined on the measurement scale. The presence of intermediate states specifically near boundaries suggests either finite interface width, unresolved substructure, local mixing, or a distinct interfacial electronic state. The tendency of erased regions to recur at similar locations across pulse cycles suggests that interfaces and local morphology together create reproducible pathways for phase conversion.

## Spectral-weight transfer, intermediate states, and the meaning of a mixed spectrum

In many correlated materials the most informative quantity is not the position of a single peak but the way spectral weight is redistributed over an energy range as the system changes phase. In the present context this issue becomes especially important when asking whether a region that is neither clearly insulating nor clearly metallic represents a genuine intermediate state or simply a superposition of neighboring phases within the spatial resolution of the experiment.

A mixed spectrum can arise in several ways. The beam spot or effective spatial response can overlap a boundary between two regions. The state within one nominal pixel may be intrinsically inhomogeneous on smaller scales. The experimental geometry can integrate over slightly different momentum cuts within the illuminated area. A simple linear combination of a charge-density-wave spectrum and a metallic spectrum may therefore fit some data well even when no distinct intermediate phase exists.

At the same time, one should not assume that every intermediate-looking spectrum is a trivial mixture. A pulse-induced or thermally induced state can genuinely sit between two limiting configurations. In ARPES this might show up as partial gap filling without a full metallic dispersion, persistence of a lower-energy peak together with a new shoulder near the Fermi level, or a lineshape that evolves systematically with pulse sequence or temperature rather than being well described by a fixed linear combination of two endpoints.

The distinction matters because it bears directly on the interpretation of erasure. If the erased regions are simply becoming spatial mixtures of insulating and metallic patches below the resolution limit, then the appropriate picture is one of phase coexistence and unresolved texture. If, instead, they occupy a distinct and stable intermediate electronic state, then the pulse sequence is accessing a richer free-energy landscape than a two-state model would suggest. Careful comparison of line shapes, momentum dependence, and spatial reproducibility is needed before deciding between these possibilities.

## Why pre-existing metallic patches are physically important

A region that appears more metallic even before the deliberate current pulse should not be dismissed as a nuisance. Such a patch can be one of the most informative parts of the dataset because it reveals that the cleaved sample can host multiple locally stable or metastable configurations even before the writing protocol begins. The origin of such a region may lie in local stacking changes, defects, strain, self-doping, or another structural modification that changes the low-energy spectrum.

The reason this matters for the pulse experiment is that a pre-existing metallic patch may not behave like the pulse-written metastable state. It may disappear after pulsing, partly convert into a different low-energy configuration, or act as a seed that changes where later pulses are effective. If its near-Fermi-level dispersion differs qualitatively from that of the pulse-written state, then the experiment is probing more than one metallic-like configuration. This complicates the interpretation of simple metallicity maps, but it also opens the possibility of learning which microscopic ingredients favor one type of electronic state over another.

From the perspective of data analysis, such patches should therefore be treated as candidate distinct electronic classes rather than merely anomalous pixels. Their existence reinforces the need for classification based on full spectral shape rather than on a single scalar contrast metric.

## Current flow, Joule heating, and anisotropic pulse response

A current pulse through an inhomogeneous device does not deposit energy uniformly. The local current density depends on the conductivity landscape, device geometry, and existing phase distribution. The local heat generation is proportional to the product of resistivity and current density squared. The subsequent temperature rise depends on heat capacity, pulse duration, and the ability of the region to dissipate heat into neighboring material and into the substrate or contacts.

This means that when a pulse writes or erases a region, the relevant driving quantity is unlikely to be spatially uniform. Regions that are structurally similar in the microscope image can experience different thermal or electrical histories during the pulse. Conversely, regions that are far apart may respond similarly if they occupy analogous positions relative to the current path and local conductivity network.

The effect of pulse direction is therefore not purely crystallographic. A pulse along one axis can create a certain conducting pattern, and a later orthogonal pulse can redistribute current and heat in a way that tends to undo part of that pattern while creating a new one. Once a metallic path exists, it can alter later current flow by acting as a preferred conducting channel. This introduces memory even at the level of transport and dissipation. A later pulse is not acting on the original insulating sample; it is acting on a sample whose conductivity map has already been modified by previous pulses.

In interpreting the ARPES data, it is important to remember that current and heat need not favor the same spatial regions. A highly conductive region can carry current efficiently while also dissipating heat differently from a neighboring insulating region. The interface between them may therefore be the place where temperature histories differ most strongly. This provides a physically plausible route by which erasing can become strongest near the boundary between a previously written metallic region and the surrounding charge-density-wave background.

## Thermal effects versus purely electronic switching

One of the central interpretive questions is whether the erase process, and perhaps part of the write process, is fundamentally thermal. The existence of intermediate states resembling those reached during heating strongly suggests that Joule heating plays an important role. However, it is not sufficient to say that the process is thermal and leave the matter there. A current pulse differs from a slow equilibrium temperature ramp in several ways. The heating is local rather than uniform, the time profile is sharp, the cooling may be rapid and spatially inhomogeneous, and the electrical drive may influence the system in ways that go beyond simple temperature increase.

The physically correct viewpoint is therefore that the pulse creates a coupled electrothermal perturbation. The system may cross into a metastable state because local regions receive sufficient energy while cooling fast enough to avoid relaxing back immediately. Later orthogonal pulses may partially erase the state because some regions are reheated into a regime where the metastable state lifetime becomes short, yet the system again cools into a frozen non-equilibrium pattern before full equilibration occurs.

From the perspective of the data, the importance of thermal physics should make one attentive to observations that suggest annealing-like behavior, edge-adjacent changes, or progressive weakening of erasure as the local conductivity map evolves. At the same time, purely geometric or structural effects should not be confused with thermal signatures. The fact that an apparent boundary aligns with a terrace edge does not by itself prove that heat flow along that edge is decisive, though it certainly makes that interpretation plausible.

## The role of chirality, stacking, and local structural variants

Layered transition-metal dichalcogenides can host more than one local structural configuration even when the gross chemical composition is unchanged. Different stacking registries between layers, different local tilts, and in some cases different chiral or mirror-related domain configurations can alter the electronic structure. Such effects can be subtle in real-space imaging yet significant in ARPES because low-energy bands are sensitive to orbital overlap and interlayer coupling.

This becomes important whenever one is tempted to label every local spectral variation as either insulating or metallic. A local structural variant may change the spectral function without corresponding to the same kind of phase change induced by the current pulse. In some cases the main diagnostic difference could appear not in the integrated near-Fermi-level weight but in the detailed momentum dependence of a band, in the sign of a low-energy dispersion, or in weak folded features that reveal the local symmetry of the reconstructed state.

Because of this, mapping local Brillouin-zone orientation and, if possible, local chirality or stacking-related signatures is not a peripheral exercise. It is part of the core physics interpretation. Only after these structural degrees of freedom are characterized can one say with confidence that a spatially varying ARPES signature reflects a distinct electronic response to the pulse rather than a different local structural background.

## What should be expected in the data before any modeling begins

Before any formal simulation or machine-learning analysis is attempted, the physically well-motivated expectation is that the data will contain several intertwined classes of variation.

First, there should be variation that is primarily geometric. This includes rigid or nearly rigid shifts of dispersions in momentum space due to local tilt or rotation of the surface. Such variation may correlate strongly with boundaries inferred from orientation-sensitive spectral features and may or may not coincide with visible real-space morphology.

Second, there should be variation that is primarily electronic. This includes changes in near-Fermi-level spectral weight, gap filling or reopening, changes in the position and shape of lower-energy peaks, and possible changes in reconstructed bands. Such variation is the most direct signature of writing, erasing, and partial erasing of the pulse-induced states.

Third, there should be interfacial or mixed-region variation. Pixels near the boundary between two phases are expected to show either unresolved mixtures of neighboring spectra or distinct interfacial line shapes. These regions may be especially informative because they can reveal whether erasure proceeds by boundary retreat, whether an intermediate state stabilizes near the interface, and whether structural boundaries pin the location of electronic boundaries.

Fourth, there may be pre-existing anomalous regions whose spectra differ from both the initial charge-density-wave state and the pulse-written metallic state. These should be regarded as physically meaningful candidate phases or structural variants rather than as mere background disorder.

Finally, the response to a pulse should not be expected to be spatially random. Even when the pattern looks irregular, it should reflect the interplay of local electronic state, structural morphology, interface position, and electrothermal history. The central physics problem is therefore not simply to identify where a region becomes metallic, but to understand which local conditions make that transformation likely and which conditions favor partial or complete erasure during subsequent pulses.

## Concluding perspective

The correct conceptual approach to this dataset is to treat it as a coupled problem in electronic structure, reciprocal-space geometry, and spatially inhomogeneous phase transformation. The ARPES spectra encode both the local electronic state and the local orientation of the sample relative to the measurement. The current pulses do not merely perturb a static ground state; they drive the system through a non-equilibrium landscape with metastability, memory, and boundary-controlled kinetics. Structural features such as terraces, flakes, and internal orientation boundaries are not secondary complications. They are part of the physics because they can shape the local current and heat flow while simultaneously altering how the electronic structure is projected into the measurement.

A disciplined reading of the data therefore begins from the charge-density-wave reconstruction and its ARPES signatures, proceeds through the problem of local Brillouin-zone registration and structural-domain identification, and then interprets writing and erasing in the language of metastability, interface motion, spectral-weight transfer, and electrothermal inhomogeneity. Only after this conceptual groundwork is established is it sensible to ask what should be simulated or what type of data-driven model is likely to extract the most informative correlations.
